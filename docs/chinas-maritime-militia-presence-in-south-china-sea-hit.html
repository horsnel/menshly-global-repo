Background and Context

The South China Sea has long been a contentious region, with multiple countries claiming sovereignty over the area. China's maritime militia, a paramilitary force operating under the guise of civilian fishing vessels, has been a key component of the country's strategy to assert its dominance in the region. The increased presence of these vessels in 2025, as reported by the US-based monitor, suggests a deliberate attempt by China to bolster its claims and exert control over the area. This move is likely to escalate tensions with neighboring countries, including the Philippines, Vietnam, and Malaysia, which also have claims in the region.

Risk Analysis

The heightened presence of China's maritime militia in the South China Sea poses significant risks to regional stability and global trade. The potential for miscalculation or accidental conflict between Chinese vessels and those of other nations is increased, which could have far-reaching consequences. The risks associated with this development can be categorized into three main areas: geopolitical, economic, and environmental.

The increased presence of China's maritime militia in the South China Sea has significant geopolitical implications. The move is likely to be perceived as a provocation by other claimant countries, which could lead to an escalation of tensions and potentially even conflict. The risks of miscalculation or accidental conflict are increased, which could have severe consequences for regional stability and global security.
